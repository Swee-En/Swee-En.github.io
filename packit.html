Pack-It!

A user research and UI design project for the UI/UX module

Project Overview

With travel as the selected theme, my team conducted research on the possible difficulties faced in this area. We realised that packing is a very common but mostly untargeted problem. Hence, with packing difficulties as our main focus, we were to design an application to ease the packing process.

Task

Design an application

Time

8 weeks

Skills

User research, Prototyping, User Testing

Tools

Pen & Paper, Tablet, Figma

Design Process

Research

Market Reseach

In order to learn about the demographics and users who would use the packing application, our team conducted market research with the help of Google. This was to help us understand who faced difficulties with packing, what problems they encountered, and why they encountered such problems.

User Research

After the market research had helped to narrow down our target users and provide some understanding on the general user needs, our team conducted our own survey. This survey allowed us to be aware of the user needs specific to our target group, which was young adults.

User Persona

Gathering all the data obtained from both the market research and user research, our team created several personas. This was to enable us to see the from the users' perspective and shape our product design. It also allowed us to prioritize certain features based on the needs of the primary personas.

Competitve Analysis

The next step was to perform a competitve analysis on the existing solutions on the market. For this, our team downloaded and tried four different packing applications to take a closer look at how the other competitors have tried to mitigate this problem. We also visited the review pages on the App Store and Google Play Store to gain an insight on the features that the users thought were useful, and the areas that could be improved on. The strengths and weaknesses of each application were then compiled into a table as shown below.

Define

Upon analysis of our research, we realised that travellers who encounter difficulties when packing generally face these two main problems:

Not knowing what and how many items to pack

Taking longer than they can / want to pack

Research showed that many users overpack as a result of not knowing how many items to bring on the trip, and despite that, they still find themselves lacking certain items. Some users also struggle with the time taken to pack their luggage, such as those who go on frequent business trips, or those who tend to pack on the day of their flight. Hence, our application should:

Help users to generate packing lists

Provide readily available sources of information to create these packing lists

With packing lists providing a clear record of the necessary items, users can save the extra time spent on fumbling around, and instead quickly grab the items as listed on the application. With sources of information giving users a guide on the necessary items and the quantity to pack, users can reduce the risk of missing certain items as well as overpacking.

Ideation

This is where we brainstorm for the features that can help to attain the goals mentioned above. Our research showed that while some of the existing packing applications provide suggested packing lists for various categories, the number of categories available are limited, and the lists generated are very general. These limitations are understandable, since the number of unique categories available are far too large, and the items necessary are also mostly specific to the users.

But what if the users can reference one another's list?

We decided to introduce a social aspect to the packing application. Just like on Instagram, Twitter, and many other social platforms, we aim to allow users to freely share their own lists online, under hashtags that correspond to that particular activity (e.g. hiking, fishing). This will allow users to be able to easily reference other lists and even add the relevant items to their own.

Prototyping

Paper Prototyping

After we had the functions in mind, the next step was to think of how we could present them on the user interface such that the application was intuitive for the users. We started off with a paper prototype where we included only the most important features, and performed user testing. This was to see if our initial ideas were suitable to be further developed. It was also easy to make quick changes, making it ideal for the initial testing.

Lo-Fi Wireframing

Next, we did the wireframing on Figma, where we designed the layout in more detail than what we did on the paper prototype. While the design was still largely in gray-scale at this stage, the flow and interactions could be clearly visualized. We also went through a second round of user testing.

Hi-Fi Prototyping

In the high fidelity prototype, we added in colors and decorative icons as well to make the application look more aesthetic and fun to use. The choice of our color palette was to let our application give off a bright energy, but at the same time have a simple and clear interface for easy use. We also included a loading screen!

Reflection

Working on this project was a huge joy. Being able to see our prototype slowly becoming complete as we add in the various features and designs was both a thrill and an encouragement.

I started off with little knowledge about UI design, but throughout the course, I learnt various things such as the 10 Usability Heauristics, as well as the iterative prototyping and user testing process.

This project allowed me to apply my learnings from the textbook and to experience the UI design process, from conducting research to producing the final prototype.
